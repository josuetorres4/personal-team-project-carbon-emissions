
# INTERVIEW 1 — Ananya Kulkarni
**Role:** Cloud Infrastructure Engineer, SaaS Company  
**Duration:** 10 minutes  

Ananya works as a cloud infrastructure engineer managing containerized applications and data pipelines deployed across multiple cloud regions. Her team focuses on maintaining high availability, scalability, and performance for production systems running in the cloud.

When asked about sustainability in cloud engineering, Ananya explained that resource optimization is usually considered only from a **cost and performance perspective**. Engineers frequently reduce idle resources, optimize compute instances, and scale workloads to lower operational expenses, but environmental impact is rarely a direct consideration during these decisions.

She mentioned that cloud providers do offer sustainability dashboards and carbon footprint reports, but these tools are **not integrated into day-to-day engineering workflows**. Engineers often need to navigate separate reporting tools to see emissions information, which means the data rarely influences real-time deployment decisions.

Ananya also highlighted that developers typically deploy workloads without understanding **which cloud regions operate on cleaner energy sources**. She believes engineers would be more likely to make sustainable infrastructure choices if sustainability insights were available during the deployment process itself.

She suggested that a system capable of **analyzing infrastructure configurations and recommending greener deployment strategies** could make sustainability far more actionable for engineering teams.

### Tool Failures Identified
- Sustainability data is **not integrated into infrastructure workflows**
- Engineers lack visibility into **energy differences between cloud regions**
- Carbon insights are mostly **retrospective rather than decision-oriented**

### Opportunity for Our Tool
Develop an intelligent assistant that integrates with deployment pipelines and **recommends greener infrastructure choices**, such as lower-carbon regions or more efficient compute configurations.

# INTERVIEW 2 — Arjun Patel
**Role:** IT Governance & Risk Consultant, Big Four Consulting Firm  
**Duration:** 9 minutes  

Arjun works in IT governance and risk consulting, where he helps organizations design compliance frameworks and transparency mechanisms around their technology systems.

During the discussion, Arjun explained that sustainability reporting is becoming increasingly important due to **growing ESG reporting requirements and regulatory pressure**. However, many organizations struggle to translate technical emissions data into meaningful insights for leadership teams.

He noted that most sustainability platforms generate **highly technical dashboards** that are difficult for non-engineering stakeholders to interpret. Risk and governance teams often need simple explanations that clearly communicate what the numbers mean and what actions can reduce emissions.

Arjun also emphasized the importance of **transparency and auditability**. For governance teams, it is essential to understand how emissions calculations were generated, what data sources were used, and how reliable the results are.

He suggested that a more effective system would focus not only on measuring emissions but also on **explaining them clearly and linking them to actionable decisions**.

### Tool Failures Identified
- Sustainability tools are **too technical for governance stakeholders**
- Limited transparency in **how emissions calculations are generated**
- Difficulty translating emissions data into **strategic or operational actions**

### Opportunity for Our Tool
Create an AI-powered interface that **translates complex emissions data into clear, explainable insights**, while maintaining transparent audit trails suitable for governance and compliance contexts.

# INTERVIEW 3 — Neha Gupta
**Role:** Software Engineer, AI Startup  
**Duration:** 8 minutes  

Neha works as a backend software engineer supporting machine learning pipelines and data processing systems used to train AI models.

When discussing sustainability in AI development, Neha mentioned that **AI workloads can be extremely compute-intensive**, especially during model training or large-scale experimentation. However, developers rarely track the environmental impact of these computational processes.

Engineering teams typically focus on improving model accuracy and reducing training time, while **energy consumption and carbon emissions remain largely invisible metrics** during development.

Neha explained that developers might become more conscious of sustainability if they could see **how their technical decisions influence energy consumption**, such as repeatedly retraining models or running unnecessary experiments.

She suggested that sustainability insights would be most effective if they were integrated into existing development tools, providing **simple feedback or recommendations during experimentation** rather than complex sustainability reports.

### Tool Failures Identified
- AI developers lack visibility into **energy usage of model training**
- Sustainability metrics are **not integrated into ML workflows**
- Developers receive **no feedback encouraging efficient experimentation**

### Opportunity for Our Tool
Design a system that integrates with AI development workflows and provides **real-time insights into the environmental impact of compute workloads**, along with recommendations for more efficient experimentation strategies.

---

# Key Takeaway

Across all three interviews, a consistent insight emerged: while sustainability data may exist, it rarely helps people **make better decisions in real time**. Engineers, governance professionals, and AI developers all expressed the need for a system that goes beyond reporting and dashboards.

Instead of simply presenting emissions metrics, participants indicated that a useful solution would act as an **AI sustainability advisor** — a system capable of analyzing infrastructure and workloads, recommending more sustainable alternatives, and clearly explaining **why those decisions are better and how much energy or carbon could be saved**.

This suggests a strong opportunity for an **AI agent that not only automates sustainability analysis but also guides decision-making with transparent reasoning and measurable environmental impact**.
