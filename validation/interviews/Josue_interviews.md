# Speed Dating Interview #1

Participant: Sofia Soberanis (Nursing Student at UofI)  

---

### Q1: Have you ever thought about how your daily activities might affect carbon emissions or the environment?

**A:**  
Yes, mostly when it comes to things like driving or electricity usage. I know things like transportation and energy use impact the environment, but I don’t usually think about the exact carbon emissions from my daily activities.

---

### Q2: Have you ever tried to calculate your personal carbon footprint? Why or why not?

**A:**  
No, I haven’t. I’ve seen carbon footprint calculators online before, but I never tried them because they seemed like they required a lot of detailed information that I probably wouldn’t have.

---

### Q3: If you wanted to estimate your environmental impact, what kind of information do you think you would need?

**A:**  
I think things like how much electricity I use, how often I drive, what type of car I have, and maybe things like flights or food choices. I’m not exactly sure how those translate into emissions though.

---

### Q4: What would make a tool like this easy or convenient for you to use?

**A:**  
It would need to be simple and quick. I would prefer something where I can enter a few basic things like my commute distance or electricity bill instead of needing exact numbers for everything.

---

### Q5: What concerns would you have about using a carbon emissions calculator or AI tool?

**A:**  
My main concern would be whether the results are actually accurate. I’d also want to know where the data or calculations are coming from so I can trust the results.

---

### Q6: Would you be more interested in a tool that only shows your emissions, or one that also suggests ways to reduce them?

**A:**  
I would definitely want suggestions on how to reduce them. Just seeing a number wouldn’t be very helpful unless the tool also told me what changes I could make.



---

### Q7: What would make you trust a carbon emissions tool enough to actually use it?

**A:**  
I would trust it more if it explained how the calculations work and showed the assumptions it used. Seeing sources or explanations for the data would make it feel more reliable.


# Speed Dating Interview #2

Participant: Jose Lopez (Nueroscience Student at UofI)  

---

### Q1: Have you ever thought about how your daily activities might affect carbon emissions or the environment?

**A:**  
Sometimes, especially when I hear about climate change or sustainability topics in the news. I usually think about things like driving less or recycling, but I don’t really know how much impact those things actually have in terms of carbon emissions.

---

### Q2: Have you ever tried to calculate your personal carbon footprint? Why or why not?

**A:**  
No, I haven’t tried calculating it before. I think part of the reason is that I wouldn’t know where to start, and I assume the process might take a lot of time or require information I don’t track regularly.

---

### Q3: If you wanted to estimate your environmental impact, what kind of information do you think you would need?

**A:**  
Probably information about how often I drive, how much electricity my apartment uses, and maybe things like travel or flights. I’m not sure how things like food or shopping would factor into it though.

---

### Q4: What would make a tool like this easy or convenient for you to use?

**A:**  
It would be helpful if the tool asked simple questions instead of requiring exact numbers. For example, something like “How often do you drive?” or “Do you live in an apartment or house?” rather than asking for detailed energy data.

---

### Q5: What concerns would you have about using a carbon emissions calculator or AI tool?

**A:**  
I’d mostly worry about whether the numbers are realistic. If the tool gives me a result but doesn’t explain how it got that number, I might not trust it very much.

---

### Q6: Would you be more interested in a tool that only shows your emissions, or one that also suggests ways to reduce them?

**A:**  
I’d prefer one that suggests ways to reduce emissions. Just seeing a number wouldn’t necessarily motivate me to change anything unless the tool also explains what actions I could take.

---

### Q7: What would make you trust a carbon emissions tool enough to actually use it?

**A:**  
If the tool showed clear explanations and maybe comparisons to averages, like how my emissions compare to other people or typical households, that would make the results feel more meaningful and trustworthy.
